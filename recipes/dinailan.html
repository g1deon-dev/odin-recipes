Description

Dinailan is a traditional fermented shrimp paste native to the Bicol Region of the Philippines, particularly celebrated in the province of Camarines Norte. Unlike conventional bagoong alamang, which remains wet and heavily salted, dinailan is produced by sun-drying tiny krill (alamang) and pounding them into a dense, solid block. This specialized drying and fermentation process yields a dark brown, compact cake with a strong aroma and an intensely savory, umami flavor. Because it is traditionally prepared with little to no added salt during the initial fermentation, it relies on sun-curing for preservation, resulting in an earthy flavor profile distinct from standard Philippine shrimp pastes.

In regional cooking, the paste is most famously prepared as Ginisang Dinailan sa Gata, where the crumbled block is sautéed with rendered pork belly and simmered in rich coconut milk. Cooks incorporate generous quantities of garlic, onion, ginger, and native chilies such as siling labuyo and siling haba to balance the savory intensity of the fermented krill. As the coconut milk reduces into a thick cream and releases its natural oils, it coats the pork and shrimp paste, mellowing the sharpness of the fermentation while amplifying the spice. The finished dish is served hot over steaming white rice, offering a creamy, spicy, and deeply savory meal.

Ingredients

  • Meat & Umami Base:

    • Pork Belly(Liempo):500g to 750g, cut into small strips or cubes.
    • Dinailan(Fermented Shrimp Paste Block):1/2 to 1 cup, crumbled. (Note: If raw dinailan blocks are unavailable, substitute with standard pink bagoong alamang or balaw that has been thoroughly rinsed under water to reduce excess salt).

  • Coconut Milk & Liquids:

    • Thin Coconut Milk(Pangalawang Gata):2 cups (used for boiling and tenderizing the pork).
    • Thick Coconut Cream(Kakang Gata / Purong Gata):1 cup (added at the end to thicken the sauce).
    • Cane Vinegar(Suka):2 to 3 tablespoons (used to balance the salty, fermented flavor).

  • Aromatics & Chilies:

    • Garlic:5 to 6 cloves, minced.
    • Onion:1 medium piece, chopped.
    • Ginger:1 thumb-sized piece, minced or cut into thin strips.
    • Bird's Eye Chilies(Siling Labuyo):6 to 8 pieces, finely chopped (adjust depending on desired spice level).
    • Long Green Chilies(Siling Haba / Panigang):3 to 5 pieces, sliced diagonally.
    • Cooking Oil:1 to 2 tablespoons.
    • Sugar:1 to 2 teaspoons (to round out the savory paste).

Steps

  1. Sear the pork:In a wok or deep pan, heat the cooking oil over medium heat. Add the pork belly pieces and cook for 5 to 7 minutes until lightly browned on all sides and the natural fats begin to render.
  2. Sauté aromatics:Add the minced garlic, chopped onion, ginger strips, and chopped bird's eye chilies directly into the pan with the pork. Sauté for 2 to 3 minutes until the aromatics soften and become fragrant.
  3. Deglaze with vinegar:Pour in the cane vinegar. Increase the heat slightly and allow the mixture to boil uncovered for 1 to 2 minutes without stirring to cook off the harsh acidity.
  4. Dissolve the paste:Add the crumbled dinailan (or rinsed bagoong) into the pan. Stir continuously until the paste dissolves and coats the meat evenly.
  5. Simmer in coconut milk:Pour in the thin coconut milk (pangalawang gata). Lower the heat to a gentle simmer, cover the pan, and let it cook for 40 to 45 minutes until the pork belly becomes fork-tender and the liquid reduces.
  6. Thicken and finish:Pour in the thick coconut cream (kakang gata), sliced long green chilies (siling haba), and sugar. Simmer uncovered over low heat while stirring occasionally until the sauce thickens into a rich, creamy consistency and natural oils begin to separate on the surface. Serve hot alongside steaming white rice.
